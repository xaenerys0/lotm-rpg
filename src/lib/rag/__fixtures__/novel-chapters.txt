Chapter 1: The Crimson Moon

Zhou's first thought, on waking in a stranger's body, was that the lamp had
burned too low. The room smelled of cheap ink and gun oil. A revolver lay on
the desk beside a notebook whose last line read, in a cramped hand, that
everyone would die, including him. He sat very still. Mr. Azik had warned him
once — in a dream, perhaps — that the dead disliked sudden movement. Beyond
the window the crimson moon hung over Tingen like a wound in the sky, and the
fog moved beneath it with the slow patience of something alive. He counted
his breaths to ten, then to twenty. The body remembered things he did not: the
weight of the revolver, the price of bread, a sister's laugh. When the clock
in the hall struck two, he rose, lit a fresh candle, and began to read the
notebook from the first page, because a historian — even a dead one — knows
that every catastrophe keeps a diary.

CHAPTER 2 — Luck Enhancement

The divination dangled from his fingers on a string: a pendulum of silver,
borrowed against next week's wages. "Answer plainly," he told it, feeling
foolish. "Is the danger past?" The pendulum circled left, which the books
called denial. He wrote the result in a ledger, dated it, and added a note on
the weather, because method was the only wall he could build between himself
and the dark. Vol. 3 of the borrowed grimoire suggested a ritual of cleansing:
spring water, a silver coin, an honest prayer. He had the coin, at least. At
the chapel on Zouteland Street the night watchers received him kindly, asked
three questions about his dreams, and gave him a fourth question to take
home — whether a man who cheats fate owes it interest. He did not sleep that
night. The pendulum, hung from the bedpost, kept turning left.

## Chapter 3 - The Gathering of Three

Above the grey fog there was a table, and at the table there were three. The
one who called himself World wore borrowed confidence like a coat one size
too large. The one called Justice laughed easily and watched everything. At
the head sat the one they did not look at directly, who poured time out of a
teapot and never spilled a drop. Business proceeded by barter: a formula for
a favor, a warning for a song. When the meeting ended, each woke in a
different city with fog on their sleeves and the taste of starlight on their
tongues, and each wrote, in three different hands, the same sentence in three
different journals: the club is real, and I am the least of it.
